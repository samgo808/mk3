NUCU 2026 Program Guide 2
SUMMARY This program is a collaborative international learning experience between the University of Colorado Boulder and Nagoya University. The 2026 itinerary expands the program into a 15-day format combining Tokyo startup ecosystem visits, Nagoya-based fieldwork, university and hospital visits, rural healthcare exposure, mobility and robotics-related site visits, group research, and final presentations. The program is designed to help participants understand how entrepreneurial thinking and technology can address the social, healthcare, and mobility challenges of aging societies.
PROGRAM OBJECTIVES • Understand the demographic, healthcare, mobility, and community challenges created by rapidly aging societies. • Explore Japanese models of innovation through hospitals, startups, public-sector initiatives, university research, and community-based field sites. • Apply entrepreneurial and design-thinking approaches to real-world problems through group research, interviews, surveys, and final presentations. • Compare urban and rural contexts in Japan, including Tokyo, Nagoya, Obu, and Nakatsugawa. • Build cross-cultural collaboration skills through interaction with faculty, practitioners, entrepreneurs, local stakeholders, and peers.
PROGRAM STRUCTURE Phase Dates Focus Phase 1: Arrival and Tokyo Startup Ecosystem May 14-17 Arrival, Tokyo startup visits, cultural immersion, and transfer to Nagoya. Phase 2: Nagoya Fieldwork and Site Visits May 18-22 Orientation, Nagoya University Hospital, xR Center, STATION Ai, Tongali, NCGG, rural town visit, surveys, and group work. Phase 3: Final Presentations and Tokyo Return May 23-28 Final presentations, closing event, optional cultural activities, Tokyo return, National Diet visit, alumni reception, reflection, and departure.
SCHEDULE OVERVIEW Note for NU students: NU students are welcome to join the shaded activities in Nagoya from May 18 to May 23. Please note that some details are still subject to change.

SITE VISIT INFORMATION Nagoya University Hospital xR Center The visit to the xR Center at Nagoya University Hospital introduces participants to the use of extended reality technologies in medical education, clinical training, and future-oriented healthcare innovation. “xR” is a collective term that includes virtual reality (VR), augmented reality (AR), and mixed reality (MR), and these technologies are increasingly being used to support simulation-based learning, surgical education, patient communication, and interdisciplinary collaboration in healthcare. This visit is closely connected to the program theme, “Entrepreneurship and Innovation in Aging Societies,” because digital technologies are expected to play an increasingly important role in addressing the challenges faced by healthcare systems in aging societies. As the number of older patients increases, medical professionals must provide safe, efficient, and high-quality care while also training the next generation of healthcare workers. xR technologies can help create realistic learning environments, allowing students and professionals to practice clinical decision-making, procedures, communication, and teamwork without placing patients at risk. At Nagoya University Hospital, the xR Center serves as a hub for exploring how immersive technologies can be applied to medical education and healthcare practice. Through the visit, participants will learn how xR tools can be used to visualize anatomy, simulate clinical scenarios, support procedural training, and enhance understanding of complex medical conditions. These technologies also have the potential to improve education across different locations, making it possible for learners to experience high-quality training even when access to clinical settings is limited. For students interested in entrepreneurship, the xR Center provides an important example of how technological innovation can be connected to real clinical and educational needs. Rather than developing technology for its own sake, successful healthcare innovation requires a deep understanding of users, including patients, healthcare professionals, educators, and institutions. The visit will encourage participants to consider how digital tools can be designed, implemented, and evaluated in ways that are practical, ethical, and beneficial to society.

National Center for Geriatrics and Gerontology (NCGG) The visit to the National Center for Geriatrics and Gerontology (NCGG) offers participants an opportunity to learn about Japan’s national-level efforts to address the medical, social, and technological challenges of an aging society. As a national research and medical center dedicated to geriatrics and gerontology, NCGG works to promote the physical and mental health of older adults and to support lifelong independence through close collaboration between clinical care, research, rehabilitation, and social implementation. This visit is directly connected to the overall theme of the program, “Entrepreneurship and Innovation in Aging Societies.” Japan is one of the world’s most rapidly aging countries, and the needs of older adults are becoming increasingly complex. These needs include not only medical treatment, but also rehabilitation, mobility support, dementia care, prevention of frailty, social participation, and support for independent living. By visiting NCGG, students will be able to understand how healthcare, engineering, robotics, and policy can be integrated to create practical solutions for older adults and their families. A key component of the visit will be the tour of the Assistive Robot Center. The center was established to support the development and social implementation of assistive technologies and care robots for older adults. Its mission includes connecting technological “seeds” with real needs in elderly care, providing testing fields for robot development from the perspective of longevity medicine, promoting research in rehabilitation engineering, and contributing to policy recommendations. Through the robotics center tour, participants will gain insight into how robots and assistive devices are developed, evaluated, and introduced into real care settings. Rather than viewing robotics simply as advanced technology, students will be encouraged to consider how these tools can support dignity, independence, safety, mobility, and quality of life for older adults.
