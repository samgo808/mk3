This session (guest-taught by Nagoya University's Hasegawa-sensei, with Tanaka-sensei of [[tongali]] joining for Q&A) covers how Japan's healthcare and long-term-care systems function under the demographic pressure described in [[reiwa-era-shrinking-society]], and reframes the goal for aging societies from pure longevity to **quality of life at the end of life**.

**Opening case study**: an NHK documentary follows Dr. Ryoichi Ichihashi, a Nagoya University School of Medicine graduate who moved from hematology into home-based hospice care. His clinic runs a large (~100-person) multidisciplinary team — doctors, nurses, dentists, dieticians, a music therapist — built around patients' individual wishes rather than maximizing lifespan. One documented case: a bedridden patient's simple wish to visit a supermarket wearing his favorite hat was fulfilled through careful planning; he died at home two months later. Nationally, ~30% of people in Japan say they no longer view longevity itself as a good thing — the documentary's central provocation is "what constitutes a good death?"

**System mechanics**:
- **Universal coverage since 1961.** Everyone (residents, regardless of age/income/history) must enroll in public insurance, typically split with an employer.
- **Age-based cost sharing**: adults generally pay ~30% out of pocket at point of care; ages 70–84 often ~20%; 75+ (a separate insurance program created in 2008, auto-enrollment) pay as little as ~10%, with income-based monthly premiums. Many municipalities (Nagoya included) cover 100% of costs for children through high school.
- **Monthly out-of-pocket caps** protect patients from catastrophic cost exposure, contrasted with the US system's deductible/coinsurance/out-of-pocket-max structure, which the lecture frames as far less predictable and more expensive for equivalent procedures (nationally set base prices in Japan vs. fragmented, negotiated US pricing).
- **Long-Term Care Insurance** (introduced 2000) covers everyone 65+ (and some 64-and-under with age-related conditions); users typically pay 10–30% after an assessed care-level rating. Home-based care is the default in Japan for three reasons: patient preference (familiarity, peace of mind), lower cost than facility care, and simply not enough nursing-home beds (public facilities have long waitlists; private ones cost more).
- **Doctor freedom of practice**: Japanese doctors can open clinics wherever and whenever they choose (unlike, e.g., the UK or Germany). This increases patient access and convenience but also causes uneven distribution (rural shortages, urban oversupply) and higher overall utilization/cost, since patients don't need referrals to specialists.

**Systemic strain**: ~30% of the population is over 65 and rising; the working-age tax/premium base is shrinking; and overuse (e.g., older patients visiting for minor complaints) adds pressure. Japan's doctor-per-capita ratio is roughly in line with other OECD countries, but the pipeline into geriatrics specifically is thin — the government now offers scholarships to steer young doctors into geriatric medicine, and rural areas face acute shortages, addressed partly by having medical universities place doctors in underserved rural regions.

**Rural vs. urban divide** (raised in Q&A): urban elderly have well-organized public transit (bus, subway, train) to reach community centers and activities; rural elderly often must keep driving into old age (Japan has no upper age limit on licenses), causing more accidents — local governments are experimenting with organized small-community mutual-aid transport and autonomous shuttle pilots as alternatives. See [[nakatsugawa-agi-clinic]] for the field-trip site that grounds this rural half of the picture, and [[ncgg]] for the robotics/mobility research side.
