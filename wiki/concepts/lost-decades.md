The Lost Decades (roughly 1991–2001, though some argue the stagnation continues) followed the collapse of Japan's asset bubble described in [[postwar-economic-miracle]]. Growth fell to about 1.14%, the lifetime-employment social contract broke, and banks kept propping up failing "zombie companies" rather than realize losses — instead of innovating aggressively, Japanese firms shifted toward risk-averse consensus and job protection. The lecture frames this as directly relevant to NUCU teams' own thinking about startup risk-taking in Japan today (see [[japan-startup-ecosystem]]).

**Galapagos syndrome** is the term (coined early 2000s) for the resulting pattern: Japanese technology evolved in isolation to fit the domestic market perfectly, then couldn't compete globally — like species on the Galapagos Islands, highly adapted to their island but unable to thrive elsewhere. Concrete examples given in class:
- **i-mode phones** (early 2000s) could do email, QR codes, cashless payment, and TV streaming — years before the iPhone — but only worked within Japan's carrier ecosystem. Japan judged it too risky to push the technology global, and the iPhone overtook it.
- **Sharp** kept perfecting cameras, phones, and TV screens without pivoting, and was eventually bought by Foxconn, its own former parts supplier.
- **Toshiba** suffered a multi-year accounting scandal (inflated profits), contributing to its decline.
- **Sony** kept perfecting Blu-ray players while Netflix built streaming; Japanese automakers optimized combustion engines while Tesla built "computers on wheels."

As a result, Japan went from 32 of the world's top 50 corporations in 1988 to just one (Toyota) today. Two additional cultural factors are offered: **monozukuri** (a craftsmanship ethic that prioritizes perfecting a product before shipping, versus a "fail fast" agile approach) and Japan's large domestic market (125 million people) letting firms stay profitable serving only Japan — unlike South Korea (51 million people), whose smaller home market forced Samsung and LG to design for global competition from the start.

**The paradox, and the frame the course wants students to take away**: despite 30 years of stagnation and a demographic "time bomb," Japan has not socially collapsed — it has kept social cohesion, world-class healthcare, and a competitive (if smaller, now 4th-largest) economy. The suggested reframe is not "how does Japan grow infinitely again" but **"how does it shrink gracefully"** — illustrated with **kintsugi**, the art of repairing broken pottery with gold-dusted lacquer, treating breakage as part of an object's history rather than something to hide. This concept bridges directly into [[reiwa-era-shrinking-society]], where the demographic mechanics of the "time bomb" are covered in detail, and into Japan's current attempt at a third reinvention via its startup ecosystem ([[japan-startup-ecosystem]]).
