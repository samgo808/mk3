This page covers the conduct rules, health/insurance system, and safety protocols for [[nucu-program]]'s in-person Japan trip, drawn from the official Program Guide and a guest briefing by Sarah Westmoreland of CU Boulder's Education Abroad office (session 13). See [[nucu-2026-itinerary]] for the day-by-day schedule these policies apply to.

**Conduct and dismissal policy**: participants must follow Nagoya University's rules as well as Japanese law; the guide lists zero-tolerance grounds for immediate dismissal with no warnings — underage drinking or drunk driving (Japan's legal drinking/driving-intoxication standards are strict, and *passengers* of a drunk driver can also face penalties), any illegal drug use/possession, misuse of prescription or over-the-counter medication, theft, endangerment or harassment/bullying of any kind, underage smoking, and unauthorized photography/recording of other participants or staff. **Adderall and some other prescription medications are illegal in Japan** — students taking them must disclose this on their health and wellness form and arrange a plan in advance.

**Health insurance**: every student on the program is covered by the same international *health* insurance (distinct from *travel* insurance, which is not included and does not cover lost luggage or stolen devices). Coverage is reimbursement-based: pay out of pocket at the point of care (Westmoreland suggests budgeting up to a few hundred dollars as a reasonable amount to front), then submit a claim; the CU Education Abroad office steps in directly for anything more serious (e.g., overnight hospitalization). Coverage includes mental health care. Coverage begins a few days before arrival and lapses a few days after the program ends — students staying in Japan afterward need separate insurance. Japan's national travel advisory level is 1 (low concern).

**Emergency protocol**: Professor Brady and Samuel Goodman are students' first points of contact for any incident (not parents first — contacting parents first delays getting actual care); Brady's wife, Tisha, is present on-site as an additional contact throughout. Brady describes keeping a recurring calendar reminder with the International SOS emergency hotline number saved in multiple redundant places (phone, watch, laptop, paper copies in luggage and pockets) as a personal best practice worth modeling. General Japan emergency numbers: **119** (ambulance/fire) and **110** (police).

**Packing/logistics essentials** (from the Program Guide): passport, thermometer (daily temperature checks are mandatory — a reading above 37.5°C, or 37–37.5°C for two consecutive days, means missing that day's activities, since the program involves hospital visits), masks (5+ days worth), roughly $200 USD equivalent in Japanese cash, prescription medication in original packaging with a doctor's note, rain gear (rainy season runs roughly early June–mid July), a laptop/tablet, and a plug voltage converter (Japan is 100V/60Hz, plug type A). Dress code for hospital visits: knee-length or longer pants/skirts, closed-toe shoes, minimal jewelry, no perfume/cologne May 18–23 specifically, and tattoos must be covered.

**Money/connectivity**: eduroam is available on the Nagoya University campus and in the dormitory; internet elsewhere requires a rented portable Wi-Fi device. International cards (Visa/Mastercard/Plus/Cirrus network) work at ATMs in banks, post offices, and some hotels; Alipay/UnionPay are increasingly accepted. Meals are only partially provided — students should budget roughly ¥20,000 (~$130) per week for food, noting limited vegetarian/vegan/halal/gluten-free options in campus cafeterias and convenience stores.
