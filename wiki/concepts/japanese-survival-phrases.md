A short cultural-literacy segment taught near the end of session 12, intended as practical vocabulary for the in-person Japan portion of [[nucu-program]] rather than formal language instruction. Each phrase is taught with pronunciation practice and its social function, not just a dictionary gloss.

- **Itadakimasu** — said immediately before eating; not exactly a prayer, but a ritual expression of gratitude, especially when someone else prepared the food. Skipping it before a home-cooked meal can visibly bother a host.
- **Gochisōsama** (more formally, **gochisōsama deshita**) — said after finishing a meal, roughly "that was a great meal" / thank you for the food. Commonly said to restaurant staff on the way out, including at casual places.
- **Hajimemashite** — "nice to meet you," said only the first time you meet someone.
- **Yoroshiku onegaishimasu** — typically paired right after *hajimemashite* when introducing yourself. Literally closer to "please take care of me," but functions as a warm, socially important "let's get along / thank you in advance for working with me" — described in class as one of the most culturally loaded phrases on the list.
- **Dōzo** — "please," used specifically as an *offering* ("here you go," "please, after you," "please sit"), not as a request.
- **Dōmo** — an informal "thanks," versatile enough for quick everyday exchanges (e.g., a convenience-store cashier handing back change) where a full formal thank-you would be socially overkill.
- **Ganbatte** — "do your best" / "go for it," used to encourage teammates, e.g., before a team pitch or presentation.

The practical framing given in class: over-formality in low-stakes situations (like thanking a convenience-store clerk with an elaborate phrase) can read as slightly odd to Japanese speakers — matching the register of a phrase to the situation is itself part of the cultural competence NUCU wants students to practice before arriving in Japan.
