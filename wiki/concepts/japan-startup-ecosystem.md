This is the course's frame for Japan's attempted **third national reinvention** — after [[meiji-restoration]] and the [[postwar-economic-miracle]] — this time built around startups rather than empire or manufacturing conglomerates, as a response to the stagnation described in [[lost-decades]] and the demographic pressure in [[reiwa-era-shrinking-society]].

**The government push**: in 2022 Japan committed ¥10 trillion (~$67 billion) to the startup ecosystem, targeting 100 unicorns ($1B+ valuation startups) and 100,000 new startups. As of this lecture, only seven unicorns had been produced, and total 2024 startup VC investment was about $5 billion — the 100-unicorn target is described as very unlikely to be hit, though the initiative is real and ongoing. The government also created **startup visas** for foreign founders and is courting foreign VCs to inject capital, echoing the Meiji-era pattern of importing foreign expertise ([[meiji-restoration]]) and offering favorable loans to companies and middle-class citizens, echoing the postwar FILP financing model ([[postwar-economic-miracle]]).

**Structural headwinds specific to Japan**:
- **Exit path mismatch**: in the US, ~90% of startups exit via M&A and only ~10% go straight to IPO; in Japan it's roughly the reverse — ~70% IPO on the Tokyo Stock Exchange, only ~30% get acquired. Japan's IPO bar is comparatively low (~$100M valuation, closer to a US Series B round), meaning many "successful" Japanese IPOs are what a US investor would consider mid-stage private companies — inflating the appearance of startup success without necessarily producing large outcomes.
- **Cultural risk-aversion carryover**: the "salaryman" lifetime-employment mindset ([[postwar-economic-miracle]]) and the stagnation-era instinct to avoid risk ([[lost-decades]]) raise the question of whether newly IPO'd Japanese companies keep growing aggressively or settle into "zombie company" compliance-mode public-company behavior.
- Digital nomad / remote-work visas exist for skilled workers who can justify the visa with in-demand skills; policy openness to this has fluctuated across administrations.

**Society 5.0** is the Japanese government's official vision for the current era, explicitly numbered in sequence after: 1.0 hunter-gatherer, 2.0 agricultural, 3.0 industrial, 4.0 information society. Society 5.0 envisions cyberspace and the physical world becoming deeply integrated (AI, IoT, robotics), explicitly positioned as a partial mitigation strategy for demographic aging, labor shortages, and rural depopulation.

Concrete examples of this ecosystem in action, visited by NUCU teams, include [[station-ai]] (Aichi's startup hub), [[tongali]] (Nagoya University's entrepreneurship/education arm), and [[prevent-inc]] (a health-tech startup built around the aging-society problem space this ecosystem is meant to solve).
