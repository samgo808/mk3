The Meiji Restoration (1868–1912) is Japan's first of three historical "reinventions" covered in the NUCU curriculum: in roughly 40 years, Japan transformed from an isolated feudal society into a centralized, industrialized, militarily powerful nation-state. It matters to the course because instructor Samuel Goodman uses it as a business-school case study in disruptive external shocks, protectionism, and rapid institutional change — themes he repeatedly ties back to modern AI disruption and the [[reiwa-era-shrinking-society]] challenges NUCU students design around.

**Background — Edo period (1603–1868)**: a strict feudal hierarchy (emperor as figurehead, shogun, regional daimyo lords, samurai warrior class, peasant farmers). First European contact came in 1543 when a Portuguese ship introduced matchlock guns, which local rulers reverse-engineered within a year. Christianity spread rapidly after 1549 but was seen as a threat to the shogun's authority (everyone equal before God undermined his supremacy); it was banned in 1614. Famine and heavy rice taxation fed the 1637 Shimabara uprising, led by 18-year-old Amakusa Shiro, brutally crushed by 125,000 shogunate troops. The shogunate then imposed **Sakoku**, roughly 200 years of near-total isolation (1639–1853), with only a small Dutch/Chinese trading post at Dejima, Nagasaki. Many scholars link Japan's modern ethnic homogeneity (~97%) to this isolation period.

**The trigger**: in 1853, Commodore Perry's "black ships" arrived demanding Japan open its ports ("gunboat diplomacy"), resulting in the 1854 Convention of Kanagawa. The technological gap this exposed discredited the shogunate and helped southern domains ally with Emperor Meiji to overthrow it.

**The reinvention (1868–1912)**, following the slogan "Japanese spirit, Western technology":
- Centralized the country, pensioned off the daimyo with government bonds, phased out the samurai class (banned swords, ended stipends), sparking the failed 1877 Satsuma Rebellion — Japan's last stand of feudal military power against a modern conscript army.
- Introduced a centralized currency (the yen) and a tax system based on money rather than rice, funding the Iwakura Mission — officials sent abroad to study Western banking, law, and industry.
- Hired foreign experts (*oyatoi gaikokujin*) at high wages, then deliberately let them go once their knowledge transferred, to preserve a distinctly Japanese approach.
- Built railroads and telegraphs; modeled banking on the US and Belgium; modeled compulsory four-year primary education (including girls) on Prussia, explicitly to produce literate factory workers and a modern workforce.
- State-funded shipbuilding and textile industries were privatized to trusted families, founding the zaibatsu conglomerates (Mitsubishi, Mitsui; Nintendo also founded in 1889, originally as a playing-card company).

**Outcome**: Japan defeated China (1894) and Russia (1905) — lopsided wins against far larger armies — earning the only non-Western seat in the League of Nations, and setting up the militarist expansion covered in the follow-on lecture on the [[postwar-economic-miracle]].

**Discussion frame used in class**: was Sakoku a successful sovereignty-preserving risk strategy or a failure that caused technological obsolescence? Is stripping the samurai of stipends to fund industrialization ethically comparable to today's AI-driven displacement of workers? Students generally concluded the comparison to AI displacement is imperfect — the samurai were a small, government-mandated class, whereas AI/automation affects a much broader, less centrally directed workforce.
