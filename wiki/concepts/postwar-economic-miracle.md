Japan's postwar economic miracle (1945–late 1980s) is the second of the course's three Japan "reinventions" (see [[meiji-restoration]] for the first). After total military defeat — Japan's economy fell to 53% of its prewar size, Tokyo's population dropped from 4.5 to 2.5 million, and people were reduced to eating tree bark — the country pivoted from building a strong army to building strong companies, without the historical benefit of a reset button.

**How it was engineered**:
- **MITI** (Ministry of International Trade and Industry) picked strategic sectors — steel, shipbuilding, electronics, autos — and coordinated capital and licensed foreign technology into them, while blocking foreign companies from operating directly in Japan, so Japanese firms absorbed the technology without foreign competition at home.
- **FILP** (Fiscal Investment and Loan Program) pooled ordinary citizens' savings nationally and channeled them, via government-directed loans, into these target industries.
- **Lifetime employment ("salaryman" contract)**: employees gave total loyalty (long hours, no overtime pay) in exchange for a job for life. This social contract, alongside workplace matchmaking, also propped up marriage and birth rates (see [[reiwa-era-shrinking-society]] for what happened when it broke).

**Visionaries**: Akio Morita (born in Nagoya), co-founder of Sony, famously turned down a 100,000-unit order from US watchmaker Bulova because Bulova wanted to strip the Sony brand off the product — a bet on long-term brand building that paid off with the Walkman (50 million units sold by 1983, entered the Oxford Dictionary).

**Results**: GDP grew from $24B (1955) to over $1 trillion by the 1980s, ~10% annual growth; by 1988, 32 of the world's top 50 corporations were Japanese, and books predicted Japan would overtake the US economically.

**The bubble and its collapse**: rising land and stock values were used as collateral for more borrowing, in a self-reinforcing loop — at the peak, the Imperial Palace grounds (roughly the size of CU's South Campus) were valued higher than the entire state of California, and a Tokyo parking lot was valued higher than Central Park. Ordinary office workers could get ~$2M loans. The government raised interest rates five times in under a year (3.25% → 6%) and capped lending, popping the bubble: roughly $10 trillion in wealth was wiped out in about a year, Sony cut 17,000 jobs, bankruptcies rose 68%, and the lifetime-employment social contract broke down. Banks kept lending to failing firms rather than book losses, creating **"zombie companies"** — a term and dynamic still referenced in Japan's current startup-ecosystem struggles (see [[japan-startup-ecosystem]]).

This collapse opens directly into [[lost-decades]], the stagnation period (roughly 1991–2001, arguably ongoing) covered later in the same lecture, and its "Galapagos syndrome" of insular over-optimization (i-mode phones, Sony's Blu-ray focus while Netflix streamed) that let Japan fall behind despite having invented much of the underlying technology first.
