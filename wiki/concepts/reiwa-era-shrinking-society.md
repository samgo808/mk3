Reiwa ("beautiful harmony") is Japan's current imperial era (began when the current emperor took the throne; the lecture places the class in "Reiwa 8"), used alongside the Western calendar in Japanese government paperwork. The course uses "Reiwa" as shorthand for Japan's present-day central challenge: **population collapse**, which instructor Samuel Goodman prefers to call a "shrinking society" rather than "population collapse" (the term Elon Musk uses) because "shrinking society" leaves room for graceful adaptation rather than presupposing failure — directly continuing the kintsugi framing from [[lost-decades]].

**The demographic mechanics**:
- Fertility rate has fallen from 3.3 (1960s) to about 1.2, well under the 2.1 needed to sustain the population; Japan loses population at a rate of about 1.7 people/minute (2024: ~730,000 births vs. 1.58 million deaths).
- Real wages have been essentially flat since the 1990s (~$38K–$40K average, in USD) even as costs rise, a direct legacy of the [[lost-decades]] stagnation.
- The population pyramid is inverting: in 1990 there were about 4 workers per retiree; now roughly 1.2. In 2025, all baby boomers turned 75, officially making Japan a "super-aged society" (the "2025 problem").
- Marriage is declining sharply — only ~2% of Japanese births occur outside marriage, so falling marriage rates translate almost directly into falling birth rates. Contributing causes: the end of arranged marriage (*omiai*) by the early 1980s, the erosion of workplace matchmaking as lifetime employment eroded, the cost of marriage becoming a "luxury," and the fact that ~50% of a couple's income goes to raising a child (vs. ~30% in the US) despite 45% of the workforce now being women in a still male-dominated household-labor culture.
- **Karoshi** ("death by overwork") persists culturally even after the lifetime-employment contract weakened — e.g., NHK reporter Miwa Sato's 2013 death from overwork after logging ~160 hours of overtime in a month.

**Cultural and market responses**:
- **Ohitorisama** ("solo") culture: solo dining, solo karaoke booths, solo weddings, and even a widely reported case of a man marrying his holographic girlfriend (Akihito Kondo). Framed as technology filling a social void as human connection becomes more effortful/expensive to maintain.
- **Silver economy** products: Unicharm now sells more adult diapers than baby diapers; Itoen makes gelatinous, easier-to-swallow tea for seniors with dysphagia.
- Funeral/end-of-life services growing as a category — e.g., a boat service that scatters ashes in Tokyo Harbor (~28 people per trip) — alongside a real problem of **kodokushi** (solitary deaths going unnoticed for months in apartment buildings).
- Rural collapse: 450–500 schools close annually; an estimated 896 municipalities face "extinction" by 2040. Government responses include rural relocation cash incentives and a (mixed-success) Tokyo dating app — the lecture's diagnosis is that the real bottleneck is structural time/work-life balance, not matchmaking supply.
- **Immigration** is under 3–5% of the population; the government is quietly targeting 10%, but research cited in class finds immigration only slows, not reverses, demographic decline, and faces real cultural/linguistic barriers (Japan is ~98% ethnically homogeneous, largely monolingual in public life).

**Comparative framing**: the US fertility rate is ~1.6 (partly propped up by immigration) and South Korea has fallen to ~0.7 — Japan is presented as further along the same curve other countries are now entering, hence its use as a "living lab" for [[nucu-program]].
