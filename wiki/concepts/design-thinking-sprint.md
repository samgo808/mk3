The virtual design sprint is the methodology CU Boulder and Nagoya University student teams use, across four combined class sessions (8–11, March 3–31, 2026), to go from an open problem space to a concrete startup solution sketch ahead of the in-person Japan trip. It operationalizes the program's core instruction (reiterated in the session 12 debrief): teams are **not** trying to solve population collapse or demographic decline outright — the challenge is to "design a tech-enabled solution that helps societies adapt to demographic change," working from the specific challenges covered in [[reiwa-era-shrinking-society]] and [[japan-aging-society-healthcare]] (labor shortage, rural erosion, aging care, social isolation) rather than the demographic trend itself.

**Sprint structure** (per the session 8–11 topic outline):
- **Day 1** — Program overview, problem context, and "MonsterWorkshop" (a divergent problem-framing exercise).
- **Day 2** — "Sailboat" retrospective exercise (what's propelling the team forward vs. what's holding it back) and "How Might We" (HMW) reframing of problems into opportunity statements.
- **Day 3 (optional)** — a special guest lecture.
- **Day 4** — "Lightning Demos" (fast surveys of existing solutions for inspiration), "Crazy 8s" (rapid 8-ideas-in-8-minutes sketching), and initial Solution Sketches.
- **Day 5** — "Art Museum" (silent gallery walk of all teams' sketches), a "Straw Poll + Supervote" (dot-voting heat map followed by a single decisive vote/star), and Storyboarding of the chosen concept.

**The consensus problem, surfaced in the session 12 debrief**: because the sprint was compressed into a single semester and Nagoya University's own students had already finished their virtual-program obligations and returned to full-time in-person classes, CU teams risked finalizing votes without Nagoya teammates' input. The instructor explicitly framed this as a real-world cross-cultural/cross-time-zone collaboration problem, not just a scheduling inconvenience: American teams instinctively wanted to vote and move forward independently, while a more consensus-oriented approach would wait for every teammate's buy-in. The resolution reached in class: each team does **asynchronous outreach** (Discord, Instagram, or other personal channels) to pull Nagoya teammates into a team-internal vote before finalizing, accepting that some teammates may not respond, while still making a good-faith effort — and remaining open to pivoting the idea once teams physically meet in Japan.

**Voting mechanics**: teams place sticky-dot "votes" (as many as they like, weighted by preference) on solution sketches in a shared Miro board to build a heat map of interest, then mark one final choice with a star.

This sprint methodology directly feeds into the field research and site visits covered in [[nucu-2026-itinerary]] (surveys at [[nagoya-university-hospital]], stakeholder visits to [[station-ai]], [[ncgg]], and [[nakatsugawa-agi-clinic]]) and culminates in the final team pitches described in that same itinerary page.
