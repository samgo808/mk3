This session (guest lecture, heavily adapted from Andrej Karpathy's public LLM walkthroughs) is NUCU's "under the hood" explanation of how large language models are actually built, in three stages. It follows [[ai-model-taxonomy]] (what kinds of models exist) and underpins [[retrieval-augmented-generation]] and [[makoto-kun]] (how the course's own AI assistant is built on these same mechanics).

**Stage 1 — Pretraining.** Models start from massive scraped web datasets: **Common Crawl** has scraped roughly 300 billion pages over ~20 years; **FineWeb** is a filtered ~44-terabyte subset (language-filtered, deduplicated, adult content and PII removed). Text is converted to bytes, then compressed into **tokens** via pattern-matching (a short passage of ~1,400 characters might become ~313 tokens — e.g., "the hobbit" becomes 3 tokens). Training is then just next-token prediction: the model guesses a token, compares it to the known correct next token in the training text, and adjusts billions of internal weights/parameters (via calculus/gradient methods, not manual tuning) to make the correct token more probable next time — repeated across the entire dataset. A pretrained "base model" can produce coherent-sounding sentences but doesn't yet know how to have a conversation or say "I don't know" — it will confidently hallucinate (e.g., naming the wrong VP for a candidate its training data predates).

**Stage 2 — Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT).** Human labelers (often domain experts — a math PhD for math conversations, etc.) write example question/answer conversations following guidelines to be helpful, truthful, and harmless; these are fed into the model using special conversation-formatting tokens (an "internal monologue start/end" marker) to teach it the assistant-style back-and-forth format. Increasingly, **synthetic data** generated by other chat models supplements expensive human labeling. Hallucination reduction is trained explicitly: labelers show the model examples of "I don't know" responses for invented facts, and later models are taught to invoke a tool (e.g., web search) rather than guess.

**Stage 3 — Reinforcement Learning (RL) / RLHF.** Analogous to a chemistry textbook's answer key: the model is given a problem and the correct answer, and RL optimizes *how* it gets there — the most efficient reasoning path — rather than just the end result. This is the origin of "thinking"/reasoning models: DeepSeek's published research showed that as RL-trained accuracy improved, the model spontaneously "thought" longer and could self-correct mid-reasoning, a technique now adopted broadly (though ChatGPT hides its thinking traces as a trade secret, while some open models like DeepSeek expose them).

**Other operating details covered**: the difference between long-term "memory" (what's baked into weights during training) and short-term "memory" (what's in the current context window — feeding a book chapter into context before asking about it works much better than relying on pretraining); models don't inherently "know themselves" (self-identification must be hard-coded via a system prompt); token-based processing explains why models are bad at exact character-counting tasks (e.g., counting every third letter in "ubiquitous") in older versions, since a word may span multiple opaque tokens, though newer models increasingly offload such tasks to code execution; and putting the direct answer *before* a lengthy explanation is more token-efficient, while showing step-by-step work (chain-of-thought) is *more* token-efficient than a plain final-number-only demand at the very start for complex problems, because it mirrors how humans are taught to work problems out.

**Scale numbers cited**: GPT-2 (2019-era) had ~1.6B parameters, a 1,024-token context window, and trained on ~100B tokens; GPT-5-class models are estimated (labs no longer publish exact figures) at 3–5 trillion parameters, ~400K token context windows, and 50–70 trillion training tokens. Training hardware: Nvidia H100 GPUs rent for ~$2.20/hr or cost $25–40K to buy; the newer B200 rents for ~$3.79/hr or costs $45–50K. Elon Musk's "Colossus" data centers are cited as examples of scale (Colossus 1: ~200,000 GPUs; Colossus 2: ~500,000 GPUs, drawing over a gigawatt — more power than a mid-sized US city).
